STEP 2: Data Understanding

Data understanding is focused on covering all activities that may be involved with the problem. In the case for our manufacturing project, we focus on collecting data, examining the data and evaluating the quality of data obtained throughout all sampling procedures and sensors located within the reactor.

Before continuing the project, let's make a short introduction as to what is sponge iron. Sponge iron, also known as Direct Reduced Iron (DRI), is a highly porous and metallic product that is the result of iron ore that has gone through the process of direct reduction within a direct reduction reactor like a shaft furnace.

Within the DR reactor the iron ore experiences several chemical and thermodynamic processes such as "Redox" reaction, "Gas-solid" reactions with reducing agents, and Heat transfer. These types of processes involve not only relevant variables such as temperature, pressure and presence of chemical compounds, but also involve physical characteristics that are present within the iron ore and the final product.

Before applying any type of data models for quality control or quality prediction, we'll first have to understand the scale and range of the data. In this case, the project will take into account the production volume, raw material data, sensors within the reactor and quality control records.

NOTE: Within this project we won't consider any data quality issues like anomalies or missing data as all data is generated specifically for this project.

STEP 3: Data Preparation

Now that all data is collected, we can identify that variables come from different databases that represent different sampling points within the direct reduction process. Due to this fact, we classify the databases in different time groups as a way to help us determine the relationships that exists between the variables and the generation of the DRI fines at the end of the process.

The DRI industrial process lasts a total of 9 hours of which the pellet enters the furnace and undergoes the physical and chemical transformation that turns the iron pellet into quality DRI. For this reason, the four databases mentioned in the data understanding section has to undergo a transformation as a way to adapt to the different time periods.

For example, if we have a measurement made of the DRI at 4pm we need to compare the result with the raw material sample data that was taken 9 hours before (7 am). By classifying all variables by their time group (in relation to the DRI reduction process) we can align all databases in a way that a single pellet of raw material can be followed and associated to their respective final product.

STEP 4: Data Exploration and Visualization

Now that the data from all sources are completely aligned it is time to apply data exploration and visualization techniques in order to understand the data's characteristics, uncover potential patterns, and make informed decisions about which variables are relevant for the modeling phase.

A way to measure the strength and direction of relationship between multiple variables can be done through Multi-variable correlation which is focused on generating a correlation matrix in order to examine pairwise relationships between the variables related to the raw materials, the DR process and the final DRI product. In this project we will not only apply basic Pearson correlation metrics but also prove if there are non-linear relationships between the variables using Spearman's rank correlation coefficient.

Pearson's correlation matrix measures the linear relationship between two continuous variables, which in the case of our dataset, it is applicable to all prepared data. Pearson's correlation is commonly used when analyzing relationships between variables with continuous data, assuming that the data is normally distributed and that the relationship between all variables is linear.

import pandas as pd import seaborn as sns import matplotlib.pyplot as plt fig1, ax1 = plt.subplots(1) df = pd.read_csv('pearson_matrix.csv', header=0, index_col=0) sns.heatmap(df, annot=False, cmap="Blues", ax=ax1) plt.title("Pearson correlation (DRI process)") display(fig1)

Spearman's correlation matrix measures instead the strength and direction of a monotonic relationship between two variables. Pearson's correlation coefficient does not assume linearity between the variables, nor does it assume that the data is normally distributed, which is particularly helpful if there are exponential, logarithmic or even polynomial relationships between two variables.

The main variables we would like to focus on are those that represent either waste (Waste powder %) or DRI pellet quality (DRI purity %, Fe % and Metallization %).
+ DRI purity represent Percent reduction which refers to the proportion of iron oxide that has been reduced to metallic iron (Fe) during the DRI process, which measures how effectively the reduction reaction has taken place as a higher percent reduction indicates that a greater portion of the iron ore has been reduced to its metallic form.

+ Metallization % represent Percent metallization which is a measure of purity of the metallic DRI product, indicating how much of the reduced material consists of pure metallic iron. Unlike the Fe % metric, the Percent metallization also takes into account any impurities or non-iron components that might be present in the reduced product.

According to the Pearson's correlation matrix, the variables that have a strong positive linear relationship to waste powder (%) has been the Ore size (0.999) and the DRI purity % (0.998), which makes sense if we consider how the final purity of the product reduces the grade of waste produced. On the other hand, Spearman's correlation matrix found strong non-linear relationships with the variables K2Cr2O7 (0.792) and SiO2 (0.767), which is interesting as they represent natural impurities within the raw ore material.

Additionally by focusing on other variables that represent the quality of the final DRI pellet, we can identify moderate negative relationships between the DRI purity % and the raw material Porosity (-0.523) where a lower porosity means a lower DRI quality. Avoiding Multicollinearity through the reduction of dimensionality can be done through the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) technique that transforms correlated variables into a smaller set of uncorrelated variables called principal components without losing information carried by the original variables.
